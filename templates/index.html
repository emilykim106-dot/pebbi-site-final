The Disease

What is FIP?

FIP stands for Feline Infectious Peritonitis. It stems from a mutation of the well known virus FECV which stands for Feline Enteric Coronavirus. FECV is a very common and highly contagious virus infecting the intestinal tract in cats. Relatively easy to treat, and detect using biotechnology.

It is an extremely rare virus, and only 5-10% of cats infected with FECV encounter the mutation: A systemic, fatal form of the virus that triggers severe immune-mediated inflammation throughout the body.

It predominantly affects cats from a very early age, typically between 4 months and 2 years, although cats of any age are susceptible to the virus. FIP is notoriously difficult to diagnose because its early symptoms resemble common conditions such as intestinal parasites (worms). FIP is commonly misdiagnosed until the virus has progressed significantly. Luckily, FIP is not contagious.

Three Main Forms

01

Wet FIP

Distinguished by fluid accumulation in the abdomen or chest with rapid progression. The most common form of FIP and the easiest to detect due to its main symptom: a bloated or pot-bellied appearance. Often mistaken for intestinal parasites.

Bloated belly · Difficulty breathing
02

Dry FIP

The formation of granulomatous lesions in internal organs makes it significantly harder to detect and diagnose, though it can progress at a slower rate. Affects the brain, eyes, or kidneys.

Fluctuating fevers · Cloudy eyes · Seizures (rare)
03

Neurological FIP

The most fatal and rare form. A severe progression of Dry FIP where the virus reaches the blood-brain barrier, effectively attacking the cat's central nervous system.

Paralysis · Unresponsiveness · Seizures

Symptoms

One of the dangerous aspects of FIP is how closely its symptoms resemble common diseases and illnesses. Lethargy, pot-belly, weight loss, loss of appetite and fever can easily be attributed to intestinal parasites or other minor infections. That explains why the majority of cases are undetectable until the disease is advanced. Note that patterns vary.

Persistent Fever

Fevers unable to respond to antibiotics is one of the biggest indicators of FIP. Fevers are often undetectable without a thermometer. If your cat's ear is warm to the touch, that indicates a fever!

Weight Loss

Gradual weight loss or stunted growth in kittens, even in cats maintaining an appropriate diet. Make sure to weigh your cat often!

Lethargy

Lethargy is reduction in energy and interest in activity, particularly notable in younger and more playful cats.

Abdominal Swelling

Visible bloating of the abdomen caused by fluid accumulation, a distinct presentation of wet FIP. Do NOT attempt belly rubs as they cause major discomfort and pain.

Pale Gums

Light colored gums indicate anemia and require immediate veterinary attention. Check your cat's gums! Even though they will hate it.

Ocular Changes

Cloudiness, change in color, frequent dilation of pupils or inflammation in the eyes may indicate Dry FIP affecting the central nervous system.

Neurological Signs

Seizures, wobbling, sudden behavioral changes (that can progress into depression), and in rare cases, paralysis can occur when FIP involves the brain or spinal cord. If your cat is experiencing paralysis, they are in need of emergency care.

Loss of Appetite

Sustained reduction in desire to eat in combination with other symptoms is a significant clinical warning sign.

Treatment: GS-441524

For decades, FIP was considered uniformly fatal. In early 2018, UC Davis researcher Dr. Niels Pedersen proved GS-441524 to be an effective antiviral medication against FIP. The drug was initially synthesised by Gilead Sciences Inc. as a potential treatment for widely known human RNA viruses such as Ebola and SARS in 2012.

Mechanism of Action

Nucleosides are the building blocks for DNA and RNA. A nucleoside analogue is a synthetic, lab-made nucleoside designed to look and function like a natural nucleoside. GS-441524 is a nucleoside analogue that inhibits viral RNA replication. Its slightly altered structure allows it to disrupt or prevent rapid viral replication.

In a different analogy for those with little to no knowledge on biology, let's think of it as LEGO. You're building a set but for some reason the piece you need to continue is faulty. You try to use it anyway, but it doesn't fit properly and it prevents the rest of the model from being built correctly. In a similar way, GS-441524 is that faulty building block that interferes with the virus's ability to make accurate copies of itself once it's incorporated into the virus's RNA (the LEGO model in this case).

Treatment Protoccol

Treatment consists of daily antiviral administration either via oral formulation (pills or liquid medication) or injection for a minimum of 12 weeks (84 days). The duration of treatment generally varies based on type of FIP, severity, and the cat's clinical response.

Financial Considerations

Thankfully, treatment costs generally depend on the cat's weight, type of FIP, and treatment duration. Financial assistance is available through organizations. See the resource section below!

For Pebbi 🐾

This is my cat Pebbi, who passed away at 6 months due to the virus.

Pebbi's FIP was initially mistaken for intestinal worms. I was told that her bloated belly was a cause of intestinal worms, and was given treatment. By the time she developed pale gums, it signaled that it was more than just intestinal worms, and was diagnosed with WET FIP. Within 10 hours of her diagnosis, her WET FIP progressed into NEUROLOGICAL FIP, where Pebbi lost all movement in her limbs and spontaneously passed away by the time we reached the emergency veterinarian.

She was well bonded with my two dogs Brownie and Tori, turned into a loaf beside me everywhere I went around the house, and inhaled Churu treats within 20 seconds. She also hated this strawberry hat I crocheted for her, one I never even got to take a photo of her wearing since she dodged the hat every time I tried to get her to keep composure haha!

FIP isn't talked about or researched on enough. If this page helps at least one cat's diagnosis, that is in Pebbi's legacy!
